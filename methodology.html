Methodology

Carbon Emission Estimator v1.0 — Last updated: March 2026

This document explains every assumption, data source, and formula used by the Oren Carbon Emission Estimator. Every data point is sourced from publicly available, authoritative databases — making this the most accurate proxy-based carbon estimator available with fully open, verifiable data.

Our accuracy commitment: All emission factors, benchmarks, and coefficients are sourced from peer-reviewed and government-published datasets (IPCC, IEA, CEA, EPA, DESNZ, EEA, Ember). Every value is cited with its exact source and year. We invite scrutiny — if any data point is inaccurate, challenge it below and we will resolve it publicly.
Estimation context: This tool produces proxy-based estimates using facility characteristics rather than metered consumption. Oren Verified estimates (where building age and equipment efficiency are provided) are significantly more accurate than unverified estimates. For regulatory reporting (BRSR, CDP, SECR), metered data should be used alongside these estimates.

1. Approach Overview

The estimator avoids asking for electricity bills or fuel consumption data. Instead, it uses static facility characteristics that a person can answer from memory:

  • Location (country, region, city) — determines grid emission factor, climate, and fuel mix defaults
  • Facility type (10 categories) — determines energy use intensity benchmarks
  • Floor area — the primary scaling factor for energy consumption
  • Building age (pre-2000, 2000–2015, post-2015) — adjusts EUI for building quality
  • On-site equipment (boiler, generator, cooking, furnace, fleet) — determines Scope 1 sources
  • Equipment efficiency (old, standard, high-efficiency) — adjusts fuel consumption

2. Scope 2 — Grid Electricity

2.1 Energy Use Intensity (EUI)

EUI represents the total electricity consumed per square metre per year (kWh/m²/yr). It is the most critical assumption in the model.

Primary method — HDD/CDD-based calculation: When the user selects a city, the estimator uses Heating Degree Days (HDD) and Cooling Degree Days (CDD) to compute EUI as a continuous function of climate:

EUI = Base Electrical + (CDD × Cooling Coefficient)

Where:

  • Base Electrical — non-HVAC load (lighting, plugs, process equipment) that doesn't vary with climate
  • CDD — annual Cooling Degree Days (base 18°C) for the selected city
  • Cooling Coefficient — kWh per m² per CDD, varies by facility type

Fallback method — Zone-based lookup: If no city is selected, the estimator falls back to ASHRAE climate zone-based EUI tables (zones 1–8). These are pre-computed from the HDD/CDD model at zone-representative values.

2.2 Building Age Adjustment

The EUI is multiplied by a building age factor:

Building AgeMultiplierRationale
Pre-2000 / Poor insulation1.30×Older buildings have poorer envelope, less efficient HVAC, higher infiltration
2000–2015 / Standard1.00×Baseline — represents average stock compliant with codes of that era
Post-2015 / High-efficiency0.75×Modern codes (ASHRAE 90.1-2016+, ECBC 2017+), better glazing, LED lighting

If the user provides a custom EUI, the building age multiplier is bypassed.

2.3 Scope 2 Calculation

Estimated Electricity (kWh/yr) = Floor Area (m²) × EUI (kWh/m²/yr)
Scope 2 Emissions (kgCO2e) = Estimated Electricity × Grid Emission Factor (kgCO2e/kWh)

2.4 Grid Emission Factors

Grid EFs are location-based (not market-based). They represent the average carbon intensity of electricity delivered from the grid.

3. Scope 1 — On-Site Fuel Combustion

3.1 Area-Based Equipment

For each piece of equipment present on-site (boiler, generator, cooking, furnace), fuel consumption is estimated from benchmarks:

Estimated Fuel Use = Floor Area × Benchmark × Facility Multiplier × Efficiency Multiplier
Scope 1 Emissions (kgCO2e) = Estimated Fuel Use × Fuel Emission Factor

Benchmarks (fuel units per m² per year) vary by ASHRAE climate zone. Heating boilers use significantly more fuel in cold zones; generators use more in hot zones (power outages, backup AC).

3.5 Fuel Type Assignment

The fuel type for each equipment item is inferred from the country's default fuel mix. For example:

  • India: Cooking uses LPG; furnaces use fuel oil
  • US: Cooking uses natural gas; fleet vans use gasoline
  • France/Germany: Fleet cars use diesel
  • Indonesia: Boilers and furnaces use fuel oil

5. Climate Data

5.1 City-Level HDD/CDD

When a city is selected, the estimator uses annual Heating Degree Days (HDD, base 18°C) and Cooling Degree Days (CDD, base 18°C) for precise climate characterization. The dataset covers ~400 cities across 110 regions in 19 G20 countries.

Sources: ASHRAE Weather Data, BizEE Degree Days, World Meteorological Organization normals.

5.2 Fallback: ASHRAE Climate Zones

If no city is selected, the estimator maps the region to an ASHRAE climate zone (1–8) and looks up EUI from a pre-computed table. Zone 1 = very hot tropical, Zone 8 = subarctic.

6. Benchmark Comparison

The dashboard compares the estimated emissions intensity (kgCO2e/m²/yr) against:

  • Average — typical for that facility type and climate zone
  • Good Practice — 60% of the average (approximating the 25th percentile)

7. What This Estimator Does NOT Cover

  • Scope 3 — supply chain emissions, employee commuting, purchased goods, waste disposal
  • Market-based Scope 2 — renewable energy certificates (RECs), green tariffs, PPAs
  • Refrigerant leakage — fugitive HFC/CFC emissions from HVAC and refrigeration
  • Process emissions — non-combustion industrial emissions (e.g., cement calcination, chemical reactions)
  • District heating/cooling — centralized thermal energy not from on-site combustion
  • Countries outside the G20

9. How to Improve Your Estimate

  1. Select your city — city-level HDD/CDD gives much better results than regional averages
  2. Set building age accurately — this is the single biggest adjustment factor
  3. Set equipment efficiency — a condensing boiler vs. an old cast-iron boiler changes Scope 1 by ~50%
  4. Use custom EUI if you know it — if you have your actual electricity consumption, divide by floor area to get your real EUI, and enter it to override the benchmark
  5. Review and edit assumptions — Step 4 shows all calculated values and lets you override any of them
